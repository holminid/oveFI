Socio-Spatial Computing for Soundwalks

2024-05-30 · video

Interactive soundwalk in Vancouver’s Olympic Village.

mesomusicalcontextbehavioralcommutingcontext-aware musicpedestrian modevehicular modeland use

Vancouver is the birthplace of soundwalks, conceptualised by members of the World Soundscape Project, including Hildegard Westerkamp, R. Murray Schafer, Barry Truax and others, in the 1970s.

In 2024, I conducted two experiments that can be thought of as soundwalks, although the non-interventionism has become very much intervention due to a portable PA. Furthermore, the experience is not so much that of a soundscape but an interactive, generative score that adapts to the geospatial environment of the listeners. This is still a communal listening experience, however, and one that requires observation of the surrounding space and place. Principles of acoustic ecology are still guiding the pre-composition of the adaptive score, but through the seeming magic of spatial computing, the walk becomes an exploration of public place, land ownership and land use. It is also a statement very much aligned with Situationist concepts like dérive, détournement and psychogeography.

For this interactive soundwalk in Vancouver’s Olympic Village, participants took turns carrying the system, so listening, movement and social attention became shared parts of the score.

This interactive soundwalk uses my proprietary technology, combining geospatial computing, physical computing and procedural sound synthesis. By combining concepts from human geography, such as space syntax and urban morphology, with urban musicology and musical entrainment, it becomes possible to create musical experiences that are perceptually and physically coupled with the activity of the listener and the listener’s environment. Because we have place neurons, musical memories can now be embedded in places.

In this way, the everyday act of walking becomes a purposeful psychogeographic exploration of places and spaces. Expanding on Situationist concepts such as dérive, or drift, becomes an act of reclaiming public space through artistic expression. The détournement is achieved by transforming mundane objects, such as street furniture, into playfully interactive elements of the walk. Different areas, as determined by zoning and land use, respond musically when visited, since each geospatial and socio-spatial object in the dataset has been categorised according to human activity or perceptual weight.

Besides aforementioned Westerkamp and Schafer, the project stands on the shoulders of Pauline Oliveros, Bernie Krause, and Judith Becker, especially in relation to deep, ritual and everyday listening, sonic awareness and acoustic ecology, extending toward dark ecology and technoanimism. Places have a profound effect on us, but the connection between music and the place where it has been created is largely lost today, much like the connection between music and movement.

In this soundwalk, listeners become active participants through the sonification of their step rate and distance to objects and areas in the city. The environment, including the weather, provides an interactive accompaniment and the overall experience is embodied and situated, as an enactivist reaction to the currently dominant cognitivist paradigm. This autopoietic soundwalk can be conducted discreetly with headphones, or as a more radical intervention using a portable PA system, in which walking becomes an act of resistance that confronts the ways in which public space has become contested and commodified.

Social cognition and spatial cognition share some of the same processing networks. This overlap suggests a link between how we navigate physical spaces and how we navigate social environments. By tapping into this shared processing, the experience can foster a stronger sense of community and connection among participants, as they experience the city in a personal yet shared musical journey.

Walking is thus transformed into a political gesture, where rhythmanalysis turns the city not only into a backdrop but a co-creator of the soundscape. Its street furniture, structures, and social dynamics are inscribed into the auditory experience. This exploration of space also interrogates the relationship between land ownership and public access. The intervention offers music as both critique and a means of reclamation.
