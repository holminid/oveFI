Triadic Mutualism - Intercity Synchronicity

2026-02-20

This piece was created in collaboration with Ezequiel Peralta for PXR Conference 2026. Shown simultaneously at VIVO Media Arts in Vancouver, BC, Toronto and Kingston, Ontario, it is a networked participatory audiovisual environment in which collective movement becomes the driving force of a shared musical and ecological system. Visitors encounter a luminous field on the floor that appears responsive but not didactic. As they move, pause, follow, gather, or disperse, they begin to perceive that the environment is listening to qualities of behaviour rather than simple triggers. Musical changes emerge from their collective activity, while smaller zones allow them to engage with remote sites, summon exchanges, or influence other communities. Over time, the piece reveals itself as a social instrument and an ecological score, an emergent multi agent system whose local and global states are continuously negotiated through motion, attention, exchange, and co presence.

Across multiple sites, geographically distributed audiences can influence one another through designated portal zones that connect remote locations in real time. These cross site exchanges are framed through bioinformed concepts such as migration, diversity, metabolic balance, flock health, and resource circulation. When groups become active, coordinated, or mutually responsive, the system produces events such as tokens, exchanges, or energetic shifts that alter both the local visual field and the evolving musical form.

The project sits at the intersection of participatory installation, embodied sonification, social choreography, and systems art. It proposes music not as a fixed composition, but as an emergent property of collective behavior. The audience does not simply activate content; it enters into a reciprocal relation with a dynamic environment that listens, interprets, and responds at the scale of both the individual and the group.

Its telematic structure extends the work beyond a single site, allowing audiences in different cities to encounter one another as abstract presences inside a shared ecological musical world. This creates a distributed public made legible through movement, rhythm, and changing systemic conditions rather than language alone. The work therefore offers a live model of interdependence: between bodies and sound, between local action and remote consequence, and between individual agency and collective form.

Biomechanical Duet

2025-07-01

Excerpt from a performance with Liliana Salazar and Ove Holmqvist at Chimerik’s Unity Consciousness 2025 festival at the Roundhouse in Vancouver.

Music and dance were once the same thing. How can we use technology to turn dancers into musicians and musicians into dancers?

In this improvisation, motion data from four Movesense sensors connected to miRack through the Holonist app. Liliana’s gestures voiced two physically modelled SWAM trumpets, while SWAM’s tenor trombone, played on a Roland Aerophone Mini over WIDI BLE MIDI through an iConnectivity A4+ interface, produced notes that interacted with the harmonic field set by the trumpets. The Eventide Qvox harmonizer was mapped to the angle of the horn, which created further brass accompaniments. A walking SWAM double bass was triggered by leg movement, and tempo was controlled by the shared average activity of all sensors, clocked via Ableton Link.

The result is not a sonification of dance but a distributed instrument in which movement, voicing, and structure are inseparable. Music and dance are once more united as one social instrument.

Socio-Spatial Computing for Soundwalks

2024-05-30

Vancouver is the birthplace of soundwalks, conceptualised by members of the World Soundscape Project, including Hildegard Westerkamp, R. Murray Schafer, Barry Truax and others, in the 1970s.

In 2024, I conducted two experiments that can be thought of as soundwalks, although the non interventionism has become very much intervention due to a portable PA. Furthermore, the experience is not so much that of a soundscape but an interactive, generative score that adapts to the geospatial environment of the listeners. This is still a communal listening experience, however, and one that requires observation of the surrounding space and place. Principles of acoustic ecology are still guiding the pre composition of the adaptive score, but through the seeming magic of spatial computing, the walk becomes an exploration of public place, land ownership and land use. It is also a statement very much aligned with Situationist concepts like dérive, détournement and psychogeography.

For this interactive soundwalk in Vancouver’s Olympic Village, participants took turns carrying the system, so listening, movement and social attention became shared parts of the score.

This interactive soundwalk uses my proprietary technology, combining geospatial computing, physical computing and procedural sound synthesis. By combining concepts from human geography, such as space syntax and urban morphology, with urban musicology and musical entrainment, it becomes possible to create musical experiences that are perceptually and physically coupled with the activity of the listener and the listener’s environment. Because we have place neurons, musical memories can now be embedded in places.

In this way, the everyday act of walking becomes a purposeful psychogeographic exploration of places and spaces. Expanding on Situationist concepts such as dérive, or drift, becomes an act of reclaiming public space through artistic expression. The détournement is achieved by transforming mundane objects, such as street furniture, into playfully interactive elements of the walk. Different areas, as determined by zoning and land use, respond musically when visited, since each geospatial and socio spatial object in the dataset has been categorised according to human activity or perceptual weight.

Besides aforementioned Westerkamp and Schafer, the project stands on the shoulders of Pauline Oliveros, Bernie Krause, and Judith Becker, especially in relation to deep, ritual and everyday listening, sonic awareness and acoustic ecology, extending toward dark ecology and technoanimism. Places have a profound effect on us, but the connection between music and the place where it has been created is largely lost today, much like the connection between music and movement.

In this soundwalk, listeners become active participants through the sonification of their step rate and distance to objects and areas in the city. The environment, including the weather, provides an interactive accompaniment and the overall experience is embodied and situated, as an enactivist reaction to the currently dominant cognitivist paradigm. This autopoietic soundwalk can be conducted discreetly with headphones, or as a more radical intervention using a portable PA system, in which walking becomes an act of resistance that confronts the ways in which public space has become contested and commodified.

Social cognition and spatial cognition share some of the same processing networks. This overlap suggests a link between how we navigate physical spaces and how we navigate social environments. By tapping into this shared processing, the experience can foster a stronger sense of community and connection among participants, as they experience the city in a personal yet shared musical journey.

Walking is thus transformed into a political gesture, where rhythmanalysis turns the city not only into a backdrop but a co creator of the soundscape. Its street furniture, structures, and social dynamics are inscribed into the auditory experience. This exploration of space also interrogates the relationship between land ownership and public access. The intervention offers music as both critique and a means of reclamation.

F.E.C. ALGO Project

2020-08-01

In 2020, I initiated the Algo project with choreographer Jonathan Valdivieso, Carla Lozano, Marlen Atty and Veronica Vasquez for the dance centre Fundación Espacio Creativo in Panama City.

The project, sponsored by Finnish sensor manufacturer Movesense, enabled 10 dancers around Latin America to perform remotely during the pandemic using my software application. The resulting video documentations feature the dancers in their lock up location, performing dances developed by the team, while motion data was used to generate music in real time for the dancers.

Technical implementation, choreography and interactive music produced by Ove Holmqvist and composed by Aleksandar Zecevic for Fundación Espacio Creativo, Panama, 2020.

Vibes #5: Interactive Choreography and Folk Music

2022-06-17

This work was developed as part of a Creative Europe–supported collaboration with Orbe and Coventry University’s Centre for Dance Research for Vibes 5, a participatory dance project designed for communal settings and audiences of different ages. The score was built as an interactive system rather than a fixed composition, allowing musical structure to respond to participants while remaining legible, accessible, and formally coherent.

The project explored how interactive music might support non linear choreography without reducing the score to reaction or effect. I created a set of responsive musical structures that could accommodate movement, changing group formations, and varying levels of participant agency. The music was composed through an expert system I designed, using musical materials that remained clear and widely graspable in participatory settings, including polka, jig, contemporary classical minimalism, and New Orleans brass band idioms.

A central concern of the work was accessibility. The score needed to support communal dance without requiring specialist musical knowledge from participants, while still offering enough structural variation and contrast to sustain interest and shape the experience over time. Rather than treating the audience as passive recipients or as isolated controllers, the project framed participation as something collective, negotiated, and musically consequential.

Vibes premiered at Birmingham International Dance Festival and was later presented at OperaEstate Festival in Bassano del Grappa. The work formed part of a broader line of research and production around interactive scores, communal dance, and systems that reconnect music with participation, bodily movement, and social coordination.

Developed in collaboration with Orbe and Coventry University’s Centre for Dance Research. Choreographic collaborators included Karen Wood, Rosemary Lee, and Scott Delahunta.

2019-01-01

Holonic Systems developed responsive performance and participatory music systems in which movement data shaped musical structure, synthesis behaviour, and, when relevant, DMX controlled lighting and stage effects through a shared logic. The work brought sound, bodily movement, and visual atmosphere into the same compositional framework rather than treating them as separate production layers.

Across these projects, gesture, posture, and activity levels were used as compositional inputs rather than as triggers for isolated effects. Movement could influence musical parameters such as timbre, articulation, density, and structural development, while the same contextual model informed lighting changes and stage behaviour. This made it possible to maintain coherence across media without reducing the work to simple synchronisation or spectacle.

A key concern throughout this work was legibility. In systems that span sound, performance, and lighting, mapping logic can easily disappear behind the result. Holonic Systems therefore treated clear signal paths and coherent system behaviour as part of the artistic and technical method. This body of work formed a major part of the development of Holon and Holonist, and of a broader practice of building participatory and performance oriented music systems in which adaptation remains authored, constrained, and intelligible.

Design

I design and build experiential systems that combine audiovisual and physical interactions, with an emphasis on participatory experiences for audiences in cultural and applied contexts. I typically support projects from concept development through production.

Case studies include concept design and branding for Telharmonium Labs and Holonic Systems, sonic interaction design, prototype design and event design for Beddit, as well as design of infographics and institutional cartography for the University of Tampere and the Academy of Finland.

Telharmonium Labs, Inc.

Telharmonium Labs develops input- and output-agnostic music systems that compose in real time from structural and perceptual models. The system delivers deterministic, expressive, and explainable scores in any definable musical form while offering a controlled and auditable alternative to black-box generation of generic audio.

The Telharmonium Composer is a rule-driven music engine built on symbolic representations of musical structure. It adapts to context and personal profiles, supports both linear and interactive composition and operates without need for custom model training.

The Telharmonium musical world model defines music as enacted temporal narrative and an emergent system state.

www.telharmonium.ca

Holonic Systems

Holonic Systems develops adaptive music systems for sonic placemaking, social musical communication, and responsive public and performance contexts. Through biosensors, physical computing, geospatial data, and mobile systems, this work explored how music could become contextually relevant, biologically and perceptually grounded, and situated in everyday life. It formed the basis for projects involving movement, biosignals, synthesis, lighting, and participatory scoring.

Holon

Holon is a zero-UI consumer demo of Holonic Systems’ platform for B2B clients. It showcases the Holonic backend for musical ubiquitous computing. Holon supports embedded audio engines, including Pure Data and miRack (iOS port of VCV Rack, exclusively licensed from mifki).

The Holon project not only challenges the visual bias in technology and urban design but also foregrounds the often under-appreciated role of sound and music in shaping human experience. Holon contributes to a broader discourse on sensory equity and on the importance of designing spaces and technologies that honour our evolutionary heritage and the full spectrum of human perception. Holon was nominated for the Music Cities 2025 Awards and the ArtTech Forum 2023 Prize.

Holon.ist

Holon.ist is the companion app to Holon and an open-world interactive music platform for emergent, mobile, and data-driven sonic experiences. It features sensor data mapping to OSC and MIDI messages, including from wearables like Apple Watch and Airpods, Movesense smart sensors and more.

The Holonic workflow reads incoming OSC, communicates with external users, devices, or services through our JavaScript sandbox, which processes and translates this data into OSM or MIDI. Holonic Systems' deep integration with standard tools, protocols and formats reveals possibilities of interactions that go beyond sonification and sound design to composition and sociomusicology and biomusicology.

Since its launch in 2019, Holon.ist has been used successfully by hundreds, if not thousands, of professional artists around the world in productions ranging from personal experiments to large-scale productions that are highly musical, theatrical, choreographic, and therapeutic. The Holon.ist mobile app, and its related plugins and services, have been central to collaborations with artists, enthusiasts and corporate clients and partners like Finnish sports technology manufacturer Suunto/Movesense and Native Instruments, Holonic Systems participation in the Bose AR beta for developers, and the partnership with mifki, the developers of miRack.

The latest Holonic component; Kaiseki, is a programmatically (OSC) controlled, tempo-synced, 8 track hot-swappable multisampler with pitch shifting and time stretching, for cloud-based structured audio and semantic music playback. Kaiseki was developed by Voxglitch and Holonic Systems to support Telharmonium Labs' mission to enable evolutionary development of any musical form. With Kaiseki, samples can be selected and loaded dynamically from live inputs like location, movement, and biometric data. The Holon.ist preset can be shared to the Holon app with the corresponding miRack patch as a very small file.

Beddit: Sleep sonification and musical alarms

Beddit developed sleep monitoring solutions and was acquired by Apple in 2017.

My work for Beddit included sonic interaction design, concept design for event productions, and audio branding services. I developed Beddit's sonic identity further in creating the bio informed wake up experience, which involved user testing and prototyping with a service designer.

The project sits within a broader line of work on designed listening and psychophysiological response, harnessing not just affective, but also behavioural and cognitive features of music. It also included public facing installation work, including an interactive booth presented at Slush 2015, which used a proprietary gluteal ballistographic cardiorespiratory monitoring system to sonify biosignals of seated visitors.

Since 2019, I have tutored PhD and Masters students in researching and developing Ubiquitous Music experiences, which are often centred around functional music, a concept we spearheaded and helped commercialise.

Field Kitchen Academy - Physical Computing & Music Workshop

The theme of Field Kitchen Academy 2020 was Curious Loops, in this case consisting of closed feedback loops, both functional feedback and biofeedback.

Participants in my workshop used Holon.ist apps and Suunto Movesense wearables connected to Ableton Live to control effects on the contact-miked instruments that they had sourced from discarded farming equipment. One performer used a Movesense with a chest strap to clock the performance from her heart rate. The sound representing her pulse was activated by her posture.
